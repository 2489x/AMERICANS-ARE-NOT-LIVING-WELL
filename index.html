2. HOUSING: STABILITY AND SUPPLY

We tackle the housing crisis with a dual approach: protecting tenants from sudden rent hikes and aggressively increasing the supply of affordable housing.

  • Moderate Rent Stabilization: Annual increases capped at 3–5% nationwide, preventing gouging while maintaining long-term housing stability.
  • Income-Based Tenant Subsidies: Direct assistance so tenants never pay more than a reasonable share of their income toward rent.
  • A Public Competitor: The federal government will fund and enable the construction of high-quality, mixed-income rental housing on public land and through strategic acquisitions. These projects will utilize streamlined federal approval to cut through the local zoning and bureaucratic delays that stifle private construction. By adding a significant volume of new units to the market, at both market-rate and below-market-rate prices, this public option will directly increase supply and create competitive pressure on the private market to keep prices in check.

3. HEALTHCARE: GUARANTEED AND AFFORDABLE

Healthcare is non-negotiable. We propose a range of clear, workable models to guarantee that every American has access to quality care, including drugs, surgeries, and outpatient specialists.

Model 1 — The Pro-Business, Employee-Choice Model

    If You Choose the Private Employer Plan:
    • You and your employer share the cost.
    If You Choose the Public Plan:
    • You pay the full cost through a payroll tax; your employer pays $0.
    The Bottom Line: Your employer's financial responsibility is limited to their own private plan.

Model 2 — The Shared-Cost Public Model

    If You Choose the Private Employer Plan:
    • You and your employer share the cost.
    If You Choose the Public Plan:
    • You pay for the plan through a payroll tax; your employer also pays a tax into the public plan.
    The Bottom Line: The public option is strengthened by shared funding from both employees and employers.

Model 3 — The Universal Support Model

    If You Choose the Private Employer Plan:
    • You pay your premium; your employer pays for your private plan and pays a separate tax into the public plan.
    If You Choose the Public Plan:
    • You pay for the plan through a payroll tax; your employer pays a tax into the public plan.
    The Bottom Line: Employers help fund the public system for everyone, regardless of which plan you choose.

Model 4 — The Fully Shared Responsibility Model

    If You Choose the Private Employer Plan:
    • You pay your premium and a separate tax into the public plan; your employer pays for your private plan and pays a separate tax into the public plan.
    If You Choose the Public Plan:
    • You pay for the plan through a payroll tax; your employer pays a tax into the public plan.
    The Bottom Line: Both you and your employer contribute to the public system no matter what, ensuring it is robust and well-funded for everyone.

WORST CASE SCENARIO PLAN: IF THEIR HQ LEAVES AMERICA

1. The Law Passes: Any corporation with over $X in U.S.-sourced revenue must pay a "Quarterly U.S. Market Access Fee" based on their verifiable U.S. revenue.

2. The Verification (The "How"):

  • For E-commerce & Digital: The company must hire a U.S.-approved third-party auditor. This auditor installs a certified software "meter" on their payment systems (like Stripe, PayPal, their own gateways) that tracks all transactions with U.S. billing addresses or IPs. This meter calculates the fee owed.
  • For Physical Retail & Manufacturing: They report U.S. sales figures, which are cross-checked with U.S. customs data, shipping manifests, and bank records of U.S. subsidiary deposits.
  • The IRS's new "Corporate Market Enforcement Division" spot-checks these meters and reports with the power to subpoena their U.S. partners' records (like the banks that process their payments).

3. The Payment (The "Daily" Effect):

  • The company is required to maintain a cash escrow account with a designated U.S. bank.
  • Based on the real-time "meter," they must fund this escrow account weekly to cover their estimated quarterly tax liability.
  • Every quarter, the IRS empties the escrow account. If they fail to fund it, the IRS fines their U.S. assets and can freeze their U.S. bank accounts.

This achieves the same goal as a "Daily Revenue Tax" but through a legally enforceable, practical mechanism. It's a toll booth on the digital and physical highways of the American economy.

THE TRADE OFF

Its a trade off that we Americans are willing to take, changing the rules of the game. From v.1 to v.2.

Version 1.0: The Unlimited Tournament (CURRENT)

The Core Rule: The same tax and regulatory rules apply to everyone, from a small business to a global giant. Success is not penalized.

The Promise: Unleashes maximum ambition. Rewards efficiency, innovation, and ruthlessness. Aims for the highest possible peak economic growth and technological advancement.

The Potential Cost: Accepts higher inequality as a natural outcome. Risks the formation of monopolies and the concentration of economic and political power. May lead to public costs from business models that rely on low wages and public assistance.

This is the philosophy of "Why should Walmart pay more just for doing well?"

Version 2.0: The Managed Ecosystem (PROPOSED)

The Core Rule: New rules—like higher taxes or stricter regulations—apply once a company reaches a certain scale of revenue, market share, or employee count.

The Promise: Aims for a more stable and equitable society. Funds a stronger social safety net and public infrastructure by asking the biggest winners, who benefit most from the system, to contribute more.

The Potential Cost: May reduce the absolute peak potential for individual wealth accumulation. Could dampen the incentive for the most extreme risk-taking by putting a "speed limit" on ultimate success.

This is the philosophy of "If you get that big, you have a greater responsibility to the system that enabled you."
