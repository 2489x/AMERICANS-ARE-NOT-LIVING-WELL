1. HOUSING: STABILITY, SUPPORT & SUPPLY

We tackle the housing crisis with a dual approach: protecting tenants from sudden rent spikes, supporting renters in both apartments and room rentals, and aggressively increasing the supply of affordable housing.

Moderate Rent Stabilization:

Annual increases capped at 3–5% nationwide, preventing price gouging while ensuring long-term stability for tenants and predictable income for landlords.

Income-Based Tenant Subsidies:

Direct assistance so tenants never pay more than a fair share of their income toward rent. Subsidies are paid directly to the landlord through secure check or ACH deposit.

Room Rental Support Program:

A simple, optional system that approves rooms at standardized local rates to prevent bidding wars or side-payment gouging. Landlords submit basic photos and a quick phone verification, and once approved, the tenant’s subsidy (if any) is sent directly to the landlord via check or ACH. The program uses light, randomized checks and landlord/tenant certification—acknowledging that some gaming can’t be fully eliminated—while still keeping the system fair, usable, and minimally bureaucratic.

A Public Competitor:

The federal government will construct and operate high-quality, mixed-income rental housing on public land and via strategic acquisitions in the meantime. Using streamlined federal approval, these projects bypass local obstruction and zoning delays. By delivering large volumes of both market-rate and affordable units, this public option increases supply and pressures private housing costs downward.

2. HEALTHCARE: GUARANTEED AND AFFORDABLE

Healthcare is non-negotiable. We propose a range of clear, workable models to guarantee that every American has access to quality care, including drugs, surgeries, and outpatient specialists.

Model 1 — The Pro-Business, Employee-Choice Model

    If You Choose the Private Employer Plan:
    • You and your employer share the cost.
    If You Choose the Public Plan:
    • You pay the full cost through a payroll tax; your employer pays $0.

Model 2 — The Shared-Cost Public Model

    If You Choose the Private Employer Plan:
    • You and your employer share the cost.
    If You Choose the Public Plan:
    • You pay for the plan through a payroll tax; your employer also pays a tax into the public plan.

Model 3 — The Universal Support Model

    If You Choose the Private Employer Plan:
    • You pay your premium.
    • Your employer pays for your private plan and also pays a tax into the public plan.
    If You Choose the Public Plan:
    • You pay for the plan through a payroll tax.
    • Your employer pays a tax into the public plan.

Model 4 — The Fully Shared Responsibility Model

    If You Choose the Private Employer Plan:
    • You pay your premium and a separate tax into the public plan.
    • Your employer pays for your private plan and also pays a tax into the public plan.
    If You Choose the Public Plan:
    • You pay for the plan through a payroll tax.
    • Your employer pays a tax into the public plan.

WORST CASE SCENARIO PLAN: IF THEIR HQ LEAVES AMERICA

Imagine this scenario: A company like Apple relocates to Ireland as “Apple Global PLC, Dublin.” They still want access to U.S. customers, so they must install a certified revenue-tracking “meter” on all U.S. payment systems—App Store purchases, iCloud subscriptions, iPhone sales, everything. The meter logs every American transaction as well as every refund or chargeback. Apple is required to keep a U.S. escrow account that the IRS can sweep each week. At the end of the week, the meter’s daily records are netted for refunds and returns, and the IRS automatically withdraws the amount owed.

If they refuse to comply? The U.S. government does not need to touch their Irish headquarters. Instead, it can:

  1. Block U.S. payments: Order Visa, Mastercard, and other processors to halt transactions for “Apple Global PLC.”
  2. Seize their U.S. assets: Retail stores, servers, and offices can be legally seized.
  3. Disable their digital presence: Revoke their license to operate on U.S. networks and require their apps to be removed from U.S. app stores.

This example shows the core principle: Access to the U.S. market requires verified revenue reporting and predictable weekly fee collection based on daily-tracked activity.

1. The Law:
Any corporation with over $X in U.S.-sourced revenue must pay a U.S. Market Access Fee calculated from their verifiable U.S. revenue.

2. How the Fee Is Paid (Track Daily, Withdraw Weekly):

  • The meter tracks all U.S. revenue activity daily, including sales, refunds, chargebacks, and subscription changes.
  • At the end of each week, these daily amounts are netted (sales minus refunds/returns) to determine the net U.S. revenue for the week.
  • Companies must keep a U.S. escrow account funded on a weekly basis.
  • Each week, the IRS automatically withdraws the owed amount directly from that escrow account.
  • If the escrow is underfunded, the IRS can impose fines, freeze U.S. accounts, or seize U.S.-based assets.

In practice, this operates like a weekly toll collected from daily traffic—automatic, enforceable, and tied directly to net U.S. sales.

THE "TRADE OFF"

Its a trade off that we Americans are willing to take, changing the rules of the game. From v.1 to v.2.

Version 1.0: The Unlimited Tournament (CURRENT)

The Core Rule: The same tax and regulatory rules apply to everyone, from a small business to a global giant. Success is not penalized.

The Promise: Unleashes maximum ambition. Rewards efficiency, innovation, and ruthlessness. Aims for the highest possible peak economic growth and technological advancement.

The Potential Cost: Accepts higher inequality as a natural outcome. Risks the formation of monopolies and the concentration of economic and political power. May lead to public costs from business models that rely on low wages and public assistance.

This is the philosophy of "Why should Walmart pay more just for doing well?"

Version 2.0: The Managed Ecosystem (PROPOSED)

The Core Rule: New rules—like higher taxes or stricter regulations—apply once a company reaches a certain scale of revenue, market share, or employee count.

The Promise: Aims for a more stable and equitable society. Funds a stronger social safety net and public infrastructure by asking the biggest winners, who benefit most from the system, to contribute more.

The Potential Cost: May reduce the absolute peak potential for individual wealth accumulation. Could dampen the incentive for the most extreme risk-taking by putting a "speed limit" on ultimate success.

This is the philosophy of "If you get that big, you have a greater responsibility to the system that enabled you."
